Description

The study of exoplanets has obtained a significant interest in recent years as a result of scientists' incredible findings. Exoplanets, regularly regarded as extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. An exoplanet is "a celestial body that is analogous to planets in our solar system and orbits around a star other than the sun," (Kavipriya et al., 2022). The discovery of exoplanets has altered our understanding of the universe and led to numerous scientific advances. This discovery has additionally aroused interest in the study of planetary system creation and development, as well as the quest for alien life. In this article, we will talk about the many types of exoplanets, the methods used to identify them, some of the most substantial exoplanet discoveries, and the consequences of exoplanet study for our understanding of the universe.

General Information

In 1619, the first group of enslaved Africans arrived in Virginia marking the beginning of slavery in the United States. Most of these people were taken from Africa and sold into slavery in the Americas, where they were forced to labor in plantations and factories. The transatlantic slave trade, which started in the 16th century and reached its peak in the 18th century, resulted in the forced transportation of millions of people from Africa to the Americas. While the institution of slavery was eventually abolished in the majority of nations during the 19th century, the legacy of this institution is still felt to this day. The desire to make a profit was the primary motivation behind the slave trade; as a consequence of it, a great number of individuals, including merchants, ship owners, and plantation owners, acquired extraordinary amounts of wealth. This paper will discuss how the founding of the United States reflected early colonists’ commitment to both freedom and slavery, and how they supported and protested against the institutions of slavery.

Abortion is a contentious issue that presents ethical and moral problems. This study will compare Christian ethics with another metaethical theory on induced abortion. Using the seven-step moral decision-making process outlined by Rae (1995), this study will argue Christian ethics as the superior ethical theory and apply it to induced abortion . Moral Decisions offer several advantages. Scott Rae argues for a transcendent, purposeful, and universal moral code, opposing the appeal to ethical relativism in current culture. According to Rae’s ethical doctrine, God’s nature, not God’s instructions, is the fundamental basis of morality. The study will demonstrate how Christian ethics addresses the subject of induced abortion and its conclusion on the practice.
